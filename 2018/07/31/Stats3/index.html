[Online Review/Statistics] Unit 3 Inference for Continuous Response Variables

Posted on 2018-07-31 | In Statistics

This unit will introduce statistical methods for comparing population means between 2 or more groups.

Overview

因predictor variable(explanatory variable/covariate): the random variables used in the model for the response variable. This is the variable we are interested in learning about through inference.
果response variable(response variable/outcome/independent variable): to explain or predict the value of a continuous random variable based on its relationship with another set of random variables. The random variable being modeled.

Road Map

Parameter: mean
Statistics: Z-score(normal dist), T-score(t-test), F-score(anova)

a T-test will tell you if a single variable is statistically significant and an F-test will tell you if a group of variables are jointly significant.

T-distribution

Conditions for using t-distribution
-Independence of observations. collect a simple random sample from less than 10% of the population.

  • Observations come from a nearly normal distribution.

Degrees of freedom (df)
describe the shape of the t-distribution. The larger the degrees of freedom, the more closely the distribution approximates the normal model. (n>=30, normal)

If the sample has n observations and we are examining a single mean, then we use the t-distribution with df = n − 1 degrees of freedom.

t-table
row: p-values (like p-values in normal distribution)
col: df
inside: T-score (like multiplier/Z-scores in normal distribution)

test statistic
Each hypothesis test has an associate test statistic. Z-score for normal dist. , T-score for T dist.

  • It compares the sample statistic to the claimed value of the parameter.
  • It is used to determine if the numerical difference, if any, between the sample statistic and the claimed value of the parameter is large enough to suggest that the difference is not due to random chance alone. That is, it is used to measure how “significant” the difference is between the sample statistic and the claimed value of the parameter.

One Sample T-Test

A one sample t-test is used when researchers are interested in making inference about a single population mean and want to compare the population mean to a benchmark value.
For example, is the mean weight of babies born to mothers who smoke less than the national average of 7 pounds?

Hypothesis Testing

  • write H0, HA
  • verify conditions for using t-distribution
  • write down μ; x_mean, s
  • compute df = n - 1
  • compute tdf= (x_mean - μ) / σ = (x_mean - μ) / s
  • look up t-table using tdf & df to get p-value, compare it with the significance value α (0.05)
  • p> α: do not reject H0; p< α:reject H0

Confidence Intervals

  • verify conditions for using t-distribution
  • write down μ; x_mean, s
  • compute SE = σ/sqrt(n) = s/sqrt(n)
  • compute df = n -1
  • look up t-table using confidence level(95% -> 0.05) & df to get tdf
  • confidence interval: x_mean - tdfSE, x_mean + tdfSE

Two Sample T-Test

A two sample t-test is used when researchers are interested in making inference about the population means from two independent groups and want to compare the population means between the two groups.
For example, is the mean weight of babies born to mothers who smoke different from the mean weight of babies born to mothers who did not smoke?

Conditions

  • each sample meets the conditions for using the t-distribution
  • the samples are independent

Paired T-Test

A paired t-test is used when researchers are interested in making inference about the population means from two paired (that is, correlated) groups and want to compare the population means between the two groups.
For example, is the mean weight of mothers’ first born babies different from the mean weight of mothers’ second born babies?

Changes
SE:

Two sample vs. Paired
Two sample t-test does not take the dependence between the two samples into account when computing the standard error of the point estimate. As a result, the standard error used in the two sample t-test is too large for a paired design which can make it “harder” to reject the null hypothesis of no difference, and we could miss a potential signal.

  • For two independent groups: SE(X̅1−X̅2)=√Var(X̅1)+Var(X̅2)
  • For two paired groups: SE(X̅1−X̅2)=√Var(X̅1)+Var(X̅2)−2Cov(X̅1,X̅2)

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

Analysis of variance (ANOVA) is used when researchers are interested in making inference about the population means from three or more independent groups and want to compare the population means across the groups.
For example, is the mean weight of babies born to mothers with the following income levels, as defined by the US government for tax purposes, different: below-poverty-level vs. low-level vs. moderate-level?

Assumptions

  • Independence. The observations are independent within and across groups.
  • Approximately normal. The data within each group are nearly normal.
  • Constant variance. The variability across the groups is about equal. Usually, the largest sample standard deviation should not be more than twice as large as the smallest across the groups.

Hypothesis

H0: μ1 = μ2 = … = μk
HA: at least one μ is different

F-test
n: number of all observations counting together
k: number of groups

MSG: mean square between groups, a scaled variance formula for means. If the null hypothesis is true, any variation in the sample means is due to chance and shouldn’t be too large.
df1 = dfG = k − 1
MSE: mean square error , a measure of the variability within the groups.
df2 = dfE = N − k.

F statistic
F statistic represents a standardized ratio of variability in the sample means relative to the variability within the groups.
F = MSG/MSE
When the null hypothesis is true, any differences among the sample means are only due to chance, and the MSG and MSE should be about equal, F=1.

  • When the F-statistic is large, we reject the null hypothesis that the group means are equal. The cut off we use for “large” for a α-level hypothesis test is the F-statistic greater than the 1 – α quantile of its F sampling distribution.

  • After a significant ANOVA signal is detected, each possible pair of the K group means should be compared using a two sample t-test and/or confidence interval. There are many

Multiple comparisons & the Bonferroni correction for α
The scenario of testing many pairs of groups is called multiple comparisons. When we run so many tests, the Type 1 Error rate increases.
This issue is resolved by using a modified significance level.The Bonferroni correction suggests that a more stringent significance level is more appropriate for these tests:
$α^* = α/K $
where K is the number of comparisons being considered (formally or informally).
If there are k groups, then usually all possible pairs are compared and K = k(k−1) .

Repeated Measures ANOVA

Repeated measures ANOVA is used when researchers are interested in making inference about the population means from three or more paired (that is, correlated) groups and want to compare the population means across the groups.
For example, is the mean glucose levels in pregnant women different during at the end of each month of pregnancy?

  • ANOVA is the extension of the two sample t-test for more than two independent groups.
  • Repeated Measures ANOVA can be thought of as the extension of the paired t-test for more than two paired groups.

Conditions

  • The observations within each group are independent.
  • The same subjects are measured within each group. This assumption can be checked by looking for any missing values of the response variable across the K groups.
  • The response variable must be normally distributed within each group. This assumption can be checked by creating a normal probability plot of the response variable for each group.
  • The variance of the paired differences in the response variable for each pair of the K groups must be the same. (sphericity assumption) The standard deviation needs to be same for all K(K-1)/2 pairs of K groups. This assumption can be checked by computing the sample standard deviation of the paired differences for each pair of the groups.Make sure that the largest standard deviation is not more than twice the smallest.

F Statistic
Repeated measures ANOVA will do the same thing, but now there will be an addition variance component in the decomposition – the variability within subjects. We
as such, we have the ability to estimate how much each subject varies about their personal mean.
ANOVA and repeated measures ANOVA are testing the same hypothesis with the same measure of “signal” but with a different measure of “noise”. In particular, the measure of “noise” in a repeated measures ANOVA will be smaller than that in an ANOVA. This is the same concept that we discussed for a two sample vs. paired t-test; the standard error of for the difference between sample means for two paired groups is smaller than the standard error of the same quantity for two independent groups because the covariance between the samples is subtracted off.

Changes on df
$df1 = df_G= k − 1$

$df2 = df_E = N − k - r - 1$

r: number of replicates in each group (that is, the unit on which the repeated measurement is made)

  • Under the repeated measures design, the pair-wise group means should be compared using a paired t-test instead of a two sample t-test. The Bonferroni correction should be used again to account for the multiple comparisons that will be made in the post-hoc analysis.

Conclusion

Inference vs. Intervals Estimation

(eg. t-distribution)

Inference: For a given sample, test how different a sample mean is from a given value to decide if the population mean is really this value.

Estimation: given a sample, calculate the population mean’s point estimate and confindence intervals

  • Mean: $μ = \bar{x}$
  • Std: $SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$
  • confidence intervals: $(\bar{x} - t_{critical} SE, \bar{x} + t_{critical} SE)$

Z-test vs. T-test

For a given sample($\bar{x}$, s), test how different a sample mean $\bar{x}$ is from a given value $μ_0$, to decide if the population mean μ is really this value $μ_0$.

If we DO know the population std σ, we could perform a Z-test.

The sample distribution is a normal distribution with:

  • center: $μ_0$
  • standard error: $SE = \frac{σ} {\sqrt{n}}$

we calculate the Z-score for this given sample: $z = \frac{\bar{x} - μ} {SE}$, get the p-value, and then compare with α.

If we do NOT know the population std σ, but we have the sample std s, we could perform a T-test.

The sample distribution is a T distribution with:

  • center: $μ_0$
  • std: $SE = \frac{s} {\sqrt{n}}$
  • degrees of freedom: $df = n - 1$

we calculate the T-score for this given sample: $t= \frac{\bar{x} - μ} {SE}$, get the p-value, and then compare with α.

For a T-test, as n gets larger:

  • $s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=0}^{n} {x_i - \bar{x}}}{n - 1}}$-> $s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=0}^{n} {x_i - \bar{x}}}{n}}$, which makes s -> σ

  • T-distribution -> Z-distribution(normal distribution)

T-tests, ANOVAs

T-tests

T-test: use how different a sample mean is from a given value μ0, to decide if the population mean μ is really this value $μ_0$.

for the sample we got:

  • sample mean: $\bar{x}$
  • Sample std: s

for the sample distribution:

  • center: $μ_2$

  • standard error: $SE = \frac{s} {\sqrt{n}}$

  • degrees of freedom: $df = n - 1$

The distribution of all samples we might get from the population. Our $\bar{x}$ is at some point of this distribution.

Paired T-test: use how different two dependent sample means are from each other, to decide if these two population means are equal. (to decide if $μ_{diff}$ is equal to 0)

for the sample we got:

  • sample mean: $\bar{x}_{diff}$
  • Sample std:$s_{diff} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum{x_i - \bar{x}_{diff}}}{n - 1}}$

for the sample distribution: r

  • center: 0
  • Standard Error:
  • degrees of freedom: $df = n_{diff} - 1$

Two sample T-test: how different two independent sample means are from each other, to decide if these two population means are equal.

  • sample mean: $\bar{x}_A, \bar{x}_B$
  • sample std: $s_A, s_B$

for the sample distribution:

  • center: 0

  • Standard Error:

  • degrees of freedom: $df = min(n_A-1, n_B-1)$ or $n_A - n_B -2$

ANOVAs

ANOVA

assume all samples have the same sample size n

Between-Group Variability: the avg square deviation of each sample mean from the total mean(grand mean $\ bar{x_G}$)

Within-Group Variability: the variability of each sample group

Why larger MSE could decrese the possibility of rejecting $H_0$

$F = \frac{variability\, between\, means}{error} = \frac{Between-Group\,Variability}{Within-Group\, Variability} = \frac{MSG}{MSE} = \frac{SSG/df_G}{SSE/df_E} = \frac{n\sum_{i=1}^{k} {(\bar{x}i - \bar{x})^2/(k-1)}}{\sum{i=0}^{k} {\sum_{j=0}^{n} {(x_{ij} - \bar{x}_i)^2}}/(N-k)}$

$df_1 = df_G = k-1$

$df_2 = df_E = N - k$

k: number of samples

n: sample size

N: total number of values from all samples = n*k

i: notation for each sample group

j: notation for each value in a sample group

t, t_critical, p, α

given a t statistic $t$ and confidencial value $\alpha$, we can :

  • read from the t-table using $t$ to get $p$, and then compare it with $\alpha$(usually 0.05)
  • read from the t-table using $\alpha$ to get $t_{critical}$, and then compare it with $t$

so

  • $t$ — $p$
  • $t_{critical} — \alpha$
